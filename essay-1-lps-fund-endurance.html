LPs Don't Fund Optimism. They Fund Endurance.

Why the best emerging managers survive cycles — not hype
AL
Anastasia Lukach
CEO Marsbase ($1.1M raised, $350M+ tokenization pipeline) · Former IR Yellow Capital ($10M infrastructure) · 1,000+ investor conversations · Dubai / MENA

Every LP call starts the same way. The GP pitches vision: AI will reshape everything. Crypto will eat finance. Climate tech will save the world. The slide deck glows with TAM charts and founder pedigree. The energy is infectious.

Then I ask: "What happens when this narrative dies? When the hype cycle turns? When your anchor LP pulls out?"

Silence.

Most GPs don't have an answer because they've never had to survive without momentum. They raised Fund I in a bull market. They're pitching Fund II before their companies have weathered a real downturn. They confuse timing with skill.

But here's what three years of raising capital and building a 1,000+ investor network taught me: LPs don't fund optimism. They fund endurance.

The numbers tell a brutal story. Crypto venture funding collapsed 72% from its 2021-2022 peak. AI hype is masking similar patterns — capital flooding consensus bets, emerging managers raising on slides not traction, everyone claiming to be "thesis-driven" while chasing the same hot deals.

I've watched this movie before. In 2021, when I was raising for Marsbase, everyone wanted in. Tokenization! Web3! The future! We closed $1.1M from angels and institutional investors — WebWise Capital, YAY Network, NovaCyber, Fractal. It felt easy.

But that's not when you learn. You learn in 2023, when the market freezes and half your investors go dark and suddenly "let's grab coffee" becomes "we're not deploying this quarter."

That's when you find out who has discipline.

Missionaries don't flee when offerings dry up.

At Yellow Capital, I built the IR infrastructure that supported a $10M raise: pitch decks that converted, partner presentations that opened doors, outreach materials that got replies. But the real work wasn't the documents. It was the relationships we built before we needed them. The LPs who trusted us because we'd been transparent through two down cycles. The strategic partners who stuck around because we delivered even when deals weren't closing.

That's endurance. Not optimism.

The data is humbling. Less than 45% of top-quartile GPs stay in the top quartile. Most lose their edge after Fund II. Why? They raise bigger funds, chase bigger rounds, compete in saturated markets. They abandon the arbitrage window that made them successful in the first place.

It's not that they get dumber. They lose their hunger. They stop doing the hard work when it's easier to coast on brand.

But the GPs who last — the ones LPs actually want in their portfolios — are doing something different. They're not optimizing for the next fundraise. They're optimizing for the next decade.

Think about it from the LP's perspective. They're not betting on your next fund. They're betting on your ability to return capital over 10-15 years. That means you need to pick well when everyone's buying, win deals when competition is brutal, and survive when capital dries up and your portfolio companies need another 18 months of runway.

Optimism gets you meetings. Endurance gets you checks.

When I was building our investor network at Marsbase, I tracked every conversation. Over 1,000 LP interactions across MENA, US, and Asia. The pattern was clear: the LPs who moved fast were momentum players. The LPs who asked hard questions — about burn rates, about what happens if the next round doesn't close, about our Plan B if tokenization regulation stalls — those were the ones who stuck around.

They weren't funding the $350M tokenization pipeline. They were funding our ability to execute on it even if the market turned.

Here's the playbook emerging managers won't tell you:

Build relationships pre-fundraise. Not when you need money. When you don't. The LPs who know you, who've watched you operate, who trust your judgment — they move fast when you're ready. Everyone else is doing diligence from scratch.

Pick a common thread and defend it. Hummingbird bet on a specific founder profile across markets. Compound bets on inflection points across sectors. NFX builds software that puts them in the data flow. Find your edge and don't abandon it when a new narrative gets hot.

Show your work in down markets. Anyone can pick well in 2021. The question is: can you support your portfolio when they can't raise? Can you make intros when you don't have social proof? Can you give honest feedback when it's easier to just ghost?

Know when to stay and when to move. The best GPs either dominate a sector (Alfred Lin and SaaS) or move early to new arbitrage windows before competition floods in. Staying too long or moving too late means you're commodity capital.

This isn't about being pessimistic. It's about being realistic. The venture game rewards preparation, not hope. LPs have seen too many GPs raise on momentum, deploy on FOMO, and disappear when returns don't materialize.

The best funds aren't smarter. They're slower.

If you're an emerging manager reading this, here's the test: Strip away the narrative. Remove the hype. Assume your next fund closes at half the size you're targeting. Assume your anchor LP says no. Assume the market freezes for 18 months.

Can you still build a top-quartile fund?

If the answer is no, you're raising on optimism. And optimism runs out the moment the cycle turns.

But if you've built relationships, if you have a durable edge, if you can operate lean and pick well even without momentum — that's endurance. And endurance is what LPs actually fund.

Because in a world dominated by fat tails and randomness, being able to predict the future matters less than being able to survive it.

The question isn't whether you can raise Fund II in a bull market. It's whether you'll still be here — still trusted, still sharp, still in the game — when Fund IV comes around and half your competitors are gone.

Tourists chase cycles. Locals build through them.

LPs fund the locals.
